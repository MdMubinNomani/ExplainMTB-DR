# Explainable Machine Learning for Novel TB Resistance Marker Discovery
Understanding how Mycobacterium tuberculosis (MTB) develops resistance to drugs is essential for improving early diagnosis and ensuring effective treatment, which ultimately helps reduce the global burden of tuberculosis (TB). When exposed to anti-TB drugs over time, MTB undergoes genetic changes and gradually accumulates mutations that can lead to resistance. Although many resistance-associated mutations have already been identified, the existing knowledge is still incomplete, making it important to develop better methods for discovering new resistance markers.

In this study, we propose xAI-MTBDR, an explainable artificial intelligence framework designed to identify mutations linked to drug resistance and to predict resistance patterns in MTB. Unlike many traditional models, this framework not only achieves high accuracy but also provides clear insights into how individual mutations contribute to resistance. This added interpretability makes the model more useful for both computational analysis and biological understanding.

To evaluate the framework, we used publicly available whole-genome sequencing data from nearly 40,000 MTB isolates. The analysis led to the identification of 788 potential resistance-related mutations, including 27 newly suggested resistance markers. Interestingly, several of these newly identified mutations are located closer to drug-binding regions in protein structures compared to previously known mutations, which suggests they may play a more direct role in the development of resistance.

Another key strength of the framework is its ability to assign scores to mutations, allowing isolates to be grouped based on different resistance mechanisms and levels of resistance. This provides a clearer picture of how resistance varies across different samples and helps uncover underlying patterns that may not be immediately visible.

Overall, this work presents a reliable and interpretable approach for detecting drug-resistant MTB. At the same time, it offers deeper insight into the genetic and structural factors that drive resistance, which can support future research and improvements in TB diagnosis and treatment.
